Storytelling Tools to Boost Your Indie Game's Narrative and Gameplay

Mata Haggis (Associate Professor - NHTV University, Breda, & Copper Stone Sea)

Monday (Feb 27) 3:50 - 4:20 PM

  • Presents charts between challenge vs. difficulty, and narrative/conflict tension vs. time
  • Both these charts have pseudo x vs. y lines with sections around them - "flow channels"
  • It is very important to stay in "flow sections"
  • Story is about change - fueled by many factors
  • Players and designers have goals for game
  • Allow story and events to be built to, give all things meaning by contrasting it to past
  • World is built -> inciting incident -> player exploration -> player acts with intention -> challenges and complexity -> rise to action -> final choice -> end
  • Most stories follow above order but you may want to add introduction (possibly a "bite of power" to rebuild back to later) or a looming ending
  • Each scene has: objective, conflict, and outcome
  • Create rewarding experiences through "valleys of conflict"

Making "Night In the Woods" Better With Open Source

Jon Manning (Secret Lab)

Tuesday (Feb 28) 3-3:30 PM

  • Dialogue: Nodes vs. Scripts
    • Nodes - intuitive but can get large
    • Scripts - Complex
  • Twine - tool for writing dialogue in game
    • Yarn - editor for Twine, open sourced
    • Yarn Spinner - more robust version made
  • Open Source: community contributions; useful to people; it lasts without an author
  • Open source services: Github, Gitlab, Bitbucket, etc.
  • There are many important parts to setting up an open source project and need consideration:
  • Have a Clearly Defined License
    • Copyleft - make open source in turn if you reuse the code/work (ex: GPL)
    • Permissive - requires attribution and allows closed source
  • Have a Contributor's Guide
    • Methods to log bugs, find help, get started, code of conduct, etc.
    • Places for discussion - IRC, Slack, etc.
  • List of Low-Hanging Fruit
    • Easy tasks for contributors to quickly finish
    • Allows you to add user-desired features
    • Important to be attentive to contributors
    • Allows first comits for contributors to feel welcome to project
  • Have a Code of Conduct
    • Dealing with conflicts minimized tomitigate discussions on ludicrous stuff/distractions
    • Allow others to police and prevent bad from good people and preserve image to future contributors
    • "Contributor Covenant" - Accept help and offload burdens down to contributors/admins
  • Takeaways
    • Make first step easy.
    • Marketing - ask around and encourage others
  • Note: Whole presentation and slides were done in game engine

Low Poly Modeling: Style Through Economy

Ethan Redd

Friday (Mar 3) 3:20 - 3:50 PM

  • Working on: Blazing Legion Ignition, NE_01, and Radbird Rally (?)
  • Low Poly - discipline/aesthetic of "surface economy"; lo-fi to get point across
    • Subjective definition ("5k per asset" - rough definition)
    • Very hard to make high fidelity 3D assets for indie developers
    • Therefore making low res assets are incentivized to save time and money
  • Benefits
    • Productivity - quicker to produce and build; allows you to make more assets with your time
    • Computational Efficiency - can "move smoother" and work with many assets at once on screen as well as on many gamers' computers
  • Thinking the "Low Poly Way"
    1. "Stylish Compatability" - design aesthetics to suit game; don't design assets that don't fit
    2. "Resolution Conciousness" - add consistency to all assets
    3. Consider Silhouette Always - assets need to be seen from all angles
    4. Render Tricks - particles, etc. Shaders are very important
  • Approximation - need to have enough details to lower "resolution" but not too far to be noticeably obscured
  • "Atomic Consistency"
    • Using larger objects can allow for different sizes of polygons
    • Scales allow for different "atom size" of composite polygons
  • "Fitting"
    • Rough vs. Accurate
    • "Dispersal" vs. "Accumulation"
  • Differentiate approaches to show details or highlight different aspects
  • Silhouette Conciousness
    1. How legible is the overall shape?
    2. How will light/shadow interact with it?
    3. Over/under selling curvatures?
  • When reducing, take care to simplify tolower geometries - personal touchto reduce without losing quality and clarity
  • "Cheating" - lightning round hacks to help
    • Low Resolution Rendering - helps hide error in low poly details and forgives certain graphical bugs
    • Color Palettes as Textures - simpler palette helps cohesion as well as rendering
    • "Fun With Normals"
    • "Scrolling UVs" - atmospheric effects
